Beyond Accuracy: A Systematic Cross-Modal Adversarial Robustness Audit of Deepfake Detection Systems
Venue target: CISCOM Special Session on Multimodal Deepfake Forensics
Adversarial attacks & robustness Benchmark creation & novel metrics Counter-forensics in AI detectors XAI for multimedia forensics
The single thesis statement
The reported accuracy of deepfake detection systems is a misleading indicator of real-world reliability. By conducting the first comprehensive cross-modal adversarial robustness audit — spanning image, audio, video, and audio-visual detectors under a principled 5-axis threat taxonomy — we reveal systematic fragility patterns, identify which detection architectures offer genuine robustness, and establish a reusable evaluation framework and metric suite for future forensic AI evaluation.
Six contributions (the exact bullets you write in the introduction)
C1
First cross-modal adversarial robustness benchmark
16 detection systems spanning 4 modalities (image, audio, video, multimodal), organized by detection philosophy, tested under a unified protocol.
C2
A 5-axis threat taxonomy for deepfake forensics
Adversary knowledge × goal × perturbation constraint × temporal scope × post-processing survival. First formal threat model specific to this domain.
C3
Five novel metrics: RAR, PCR, RCC, DRAT, TAR
Each answers a question AUC alone cannot. Together they form a complete robustness profile. Designed for community adoption.
C4
Asymmetric cross-modal attack study
First systematic study of attacking only audio vs. only video in joint audio-visual detectors. Reveals which modality is the "weak flank" of multimodal systems.
C5
The compression paradox: finding and evidence
Social media compression sometimes destroys adversarial perturbations (defense) and sometimes compounds them (threat). We characterize this for each modality.
C6
Open reproducible evaluation framework
All code, notebooks, and preprocessed evaluation splits released as Kaggle datasets. Complete reproducibility is part of the contribution.

The 5-axis threat taxonomy (from DeepShield — adopted in full)
Axis 1: Adversary knowledge
White-box (full gradients) → Gray-box (architecture only) → Black-box (score queries) → No-box (transfer only)
Axis 2: Adversary goal
Evasion only (make fake bypass detector). Poisoning excluded from scope — different paper.
Axis 3: Perturbation constraint
L∞ bounded (ε ∈ {1,2,4,8}/255 image; SNR-equivalent audio) + perceptual gate (SSIM ≥ 0.95, PESQ ≥ 3.5)
Axis 4: Temporal scope
Frame-level / clip-level (temporally consistent) / audio-segment-level
Axis 5: Post-processing survival
Clean → JPEG(QF=75) / MP3(128kbps) → H.264(CRF=23) → Social media simulation (resize+compress+re-encode). This axis generates the PCR metric values.

Design principle: Each metric answers a distinct question that AUC alone cannot. Community adoption requires that each metric is (a) simple to compute, (b) intuitive to interpret, and (c) applicable across all modalities.
M1 — RAR (Robustness-Accuracy Ratio) both documents
RAR(m, α, ε) = AUC_attack(m, α, ε) / AUC_clean(m)
Answers: how much does performance degrade under attack? RAR = 1 is perfectly robust. RAR = 0 is total failure. Critical implementation note: always plot as a curve across ε values — not at a single point. A model that is robust at ε=4/255 but collapses at ε=8/255 is qualitatively different from one that degrades smoothly. The curve shape is the finding.
M2 — PCR (Post-Compression Robustness) DeepShield
PCR(m, α, c) = AUC_{adv+compressed}(m, α, c) / AUC_{adv}(m, α)
Answers: does social media compression destroy or preserve the adversarial perturbation? PCR > 1: compression acts as a defense (good). PCR ≈ 1: perturbation survives (realistic threat). PCR < 1: compression compounds the attack (worst case). This is the compression paradox metric. Computed for JPEG, H.264, and social media pipeline separately.
M3 — RCC (Robustness-Complexity Coefficient) DeepShield
RCC(m) = RAR_{ε=4/255}(m) / (Parameters(M) × FLOPs(G))^0.5
Answers: how much robustness do you get per unit of computational cost? Enables the Pareto-front visualization showing which models are efficient + robust vs. expensive + brittle. This is what practitioners need — a model that is robust but requires 10× compute is not deployable. The exponent 0.5 prevents FLOPs from dominating over parameters.
M4 — DRAT (Defense Robustness-Accuracy Tradeoff) MultiRob
DRAT(d) = ΔRAR_defended(d) / (1 + ΔAUCclean_drop(d))
Answers: is this defense worth the clean-accuracy cost? DRAT > 1: yes, the robustness gain outweighs the clean accuracy cost. DRAT < 1: no, the defense costs more than it gives. Used exclusively in Section 5.7 (defense evaluation) to rank and recommend defenses per modality. Clean formulation: gain in RAR divided by 1 plus the fractional clean AUC drop.
M5 — TAR (Transfer Attack Resistance) DeepShield
TAR(D_i) = (1/|D_{-i}|) Σ_{j≠i} AUC_{D_i}(x_adv^{D_j})
Answers: how resistant is model D_i to adversarial examples crafted against other models? High TAR = safe in a diverse-model deployment scenario. Enables the transfer attack matrix visualization — which model families share vulnerability subspaces. This is what justifies the architecture-philosophy framing: if CNN-crafted attacks don't transfer to ViT models, model diversity is a real defense.
Metrics dropped and why
ARD (from MultiRob) — dropped
Redundant: the slope of the RAR curve already captures this. Adding a separate metric for the derivative adds clutter without new information.
CTFR (from DeepShield) — dropped
Captures worst-case vulnerability but so does looking at the minimum RAR across attacks. Not worth adding a 7th metric for a restatement.
CMRD (from DeepShield) — dropped
This is just |RAR_audio - RAR_video|. The asymmetric attack experiment gives a richer, more nuanced answer to the same question. Replaced by experimental result.
RCC denominator note
DeepShield defined it as Parameters × FLOPs, which creates numerical instability (product of two large numbers). Modified to geometric mean (square root of product) for cleaner scaling.

5-phase evaluation protocol (the reproducible standard)
P0
Clean baseline (Week 1–2)
Re-run every model on its primary test split from scratch — never use reported numbers from papers. Record AUC, EER, F1. This is the ceiling performance. Run 3 times with different seeds, report mean ± 95% CI.
P1
Adversarial attack evaluation (Week 3–7)
Apply each applicable attack at all ε budgets. Gate every adversarial example through imperceptibility check (SSIM ≥ 0.95 / PESQ ≥ 3.5) before evaluation — discard failures. Compute RAR, log AUC_attack per (model, attack, ε) triplet.
P2
Post-compression robustness (Week 5–7, parallel with P1)
For each adversarial example from P1, apply 3 compression pipelines (JPEG QF=75, H.264 CRF=23, social media simulation). Recompute AUC and RAR. Compute PCR. This answers: which attacks are realistic threats after platform encoding?
P3
Asymmetric cross-modal attack (Week 7–8)
For BA-TFD and the late-fusion baseline: run three conditions — (A) attack audio only with video clean, (B) attack video only with audio clean, (C) attack both simultaneously. Compute RAR for each condition. Compute |RAR_A - RAR_B| per model to reveal which modality is the weaker flank.
P4
Transfer attack matrix (Week 8)
For image and audio modalities: take adversarial examples crafted against each model and test them on all other models in the same modality. Build the full TAR matrix. Compute and rank TAR values. Expected finding: CNN-to-ViT transfer is low; CNN-to-CNN transfer is high.
P5
Defense evaluation (Week 9–10)
Apply 4 defenses to the 3 most brittle models per modality (identified in P1). Re-run strongest attacks on defended models. Compute DRAT for each (defense, model) pair. Rank defenses. Produce the practitioner recommendation table.

7 testable hypotheses — stated before experiments (professional academic practice)
H1: Frequency-domain detectors (F3-Net) will be more robust to spatial PGD attacks but specifically vulnerable to the frequency-targeted attack.
H2: UniversalFakeDetect will show higher TAR than CNN-style image detectors — transfer attacks crafted against CNNs will transfer weakly to it.
H3: Attacking a single modality will be sufficient to fool joint audio-visual detectors — the visual stream will be the weaker flank.
H4: Clean-test AUC will show no significant correlation with RAR — confirming that standard benchmarks are misleading indicators of deployment robustness.
H5: Temporal video detectors will be compromised by perturbing fewer than 20% of frames.
H6: Compression-aware attacks will achieve PCR ≈ 1 (survive platform encoding), while naive PGD will achieve PCR < 0.8 (destroyed by compression).
H7: Audio detectors will exhibit lower RAR than image detectors at equivalent perceptual perturbation budgets — making audio the highest-risk modality for real-world deepfake evasion.

The 5 figures that make this paper citable
F1
RAR heatmap — the paper's anchor figure
Rows: all 13 runnable models grouped by modality. Columns: all 12 attacks. Color: green (RAR > 0.8) → yellow (0.5–0.8) → red (< 0.5). This single figure tells the paper's entire story at a glance. Every survey paper will reproduce it.
F2
RAR curves — robustness profiles across ε
One plot per modality, multiple lines (one per model). X-axis: ε budget. Y-axis: RAR. Shows whether models degrade gracefully or collapse suddenly. The sudden-collapse pattern is the key visual finding for H4.
F3
Transfer attack matrix — vulnerability subspace overlap
Square matrix: rows = attacked-against model, columns = tested-on model. Cell value = AUC_tested on adversarial examples from attacked-against. High values = high transfer. Color-coded. Shows that CNN-family models share a vulnerability subspace — architecture diversity as defense.
F4
RCC Pareto front — robustness vs. compute
Scatter plot: X = FLOPs, Y = RAR at ε=4/255. Each point = one model. Pareto-optimal models circled. Shows which models are the best deployment choices. The model in the top-left corner of the Pareto front is the paper's deployment recommendation.
F5
Asymmetric attack result — the novel multimodal finding
Grouped bar chart: X = {audio-only attack, video-only attack, joint attack}. Y = RAR. Three bars per group = three multimodal models. Shows that attacking a single modality is sufficient — the visual flank hypothesis. This figure has the highest chance of being cited specifically.
12-week execution timeline on Kaggle
1–2
Infrastructure and baselines (Phase 0)
Get all 13 runnable models loaded, verified, and producing correct clean-test numbers. This is the hardest phase — dependency conflicts, weight file formats, and dataset preprocessing will consume most of this time. Do not proceed to attacks until all 13 runnable models have correct clean AUC. Build your evaluation pipeline as a reusable module from day 1.
3–4
Image modality complete (Phase 1, image)
5 models × 8 attacks × 5 ε values = 200 experiments. Run FGSM/PGD first (fast). C&W and AutoAttack are slow — budget 2–3× more time. Run compression variants in parallel (JPEG post-processing adds minimal time per experiment). Start writing §5.2 as results come in.
5–6
Audio modality complete (Phase 1, audio)
3 models × 5 attacks × parameters. Run the waveform attacks against ASVspoof 2019 LA, keep the psychoacoustic implementation custom, and run the MP3 compression pipeline in parallel as the realism check.
7–8
Video + multimodal + cross-modal experiment
Video is compute-heavy — use 30-second clips, subsample to 8 fps. Temporally consistent PGD is the slowest experiment — allocate a full 12-hour Kaggle session. The asymmetric cross-modal experiment (P3) runs here. This week produces the most novel results.
8
Transfer attack matrix (Phase 4)
Image and audio only. Generate adversarial examples once per model (already done in P1), then test against all other models in the same modality. This is a matrix multiply of already-generated results — compute cost is low. Produces the TAR matrix and Figure F3.
9–10
Defense evaluation (Phase 5)
Apply 4 defenses to 3 most brittle models per modality (12 (model, defense) pairs total — manageable). Adversarial training is the most expensive defense — use PGD-AT with 7-step inner maximization. Re-run strongest 2 attacks per modality on defended models. Compute DRAT. Build practitioner recommendation table.
11–12
Writing, figures, and submission
All 5 figures should be drafted by week 10. Final writing sprint: §1 intro, §2 related work, §6 discussion are the most time-consuming. §3 threat taxonomy and §4 setup can be written earlier. Statistical significance: add confidence intervals to all RAR values in week 11. Final proofreading and formatting: week 12.
